# Brand Naming Research Notes

NamEngine Business should use naming research as quiet engine guidance, not as visible academic copy.

## Practical Engine Signals

- **Processing fluency:** Names that are easier to read, pronounce, and understand tend to feel more familiar and trustworthy. Use fluency as a positive signal, but do not let it flatten everything into generic names.
- **Distinctiveness:** A strong business name should be memorable and separable from competitors. Penalize generic keyword stuffing and obvious category phrases unless the user clearly wants local/service clarity.
- **Trademark posture:** Treat suggestive, arbitrary, and fanciful names as stronger than descriptive or generic names. This is not legal clearance and should never be described as trademark availability.
- **Sound symbolism:** Letter sounds can carry directional meaning. Crisper consonants can feel more energetic, technical, sharp, or fast; softer sounds and open vowels can feel warmer, calmer, more human, or more premium.
- **Context fit:** The best naming strategy changes by project type. A local service business may need clarity and trust; an app or product may need more searchability and ownability; a studio may allow more personality.

## Guardrails

- Avoid famous brand echoes, competitor confusion, and legal overclaims.
- Avoid empty startup cliches such as excessive `x`, `q`, `io`, `ify`, or vague `labs/ai/solutions` patterns unless the user asks for that direction.
- Avoid names that are clever only after explanation.
- Make results useful for a founder saying the name in a pitch, referral, invoice, website header, and domain search.
